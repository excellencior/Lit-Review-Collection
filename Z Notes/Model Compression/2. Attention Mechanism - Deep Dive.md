## 1. The Problem: Why Do We Even Need Attention?

### The Sentence That Breaks Simple Models

Consider:

> **"The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired."**

What does **"it"** refer to? вҶ’ The **animal** (because "tired" applies to a living thing).

Now change one word:

> **"The animal didn't cross the street because it was too wide."**

What does **"it"** refer to now? вҶ’ The **street** (because "wide" applies to a road).

The word **"it"** is in the exact same position in both sentences. A model that only looks at **position** or **nearby words** would fail. It needs to look **across the entire sentence** and figure out relationships.

### What Came Before Attention: The Bottleneck Problem

Before the Transformer era, the dominant architecture for sequence processing was the **Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)**. Understanding why RNNs fall short is essential to appreciating what attention solves.

#### What Is an RNN?

An RNN is a neural network designed for sequential data (text, time-series, audio). Unlike a standard feedforward network that processes a fixed-size input all at once, an RNN processes one element at a time **in order**, maintaining a "hidden state" vector `h` that acts as its running memory.

At each time step `t`, the RNN:
1. Takes the current input `xвӮң` (e.g., the embedding of word `t`)
2. Combines it with the previous hidden state `hвӮңвӮӢвӮҒ`
3. Produces a new hidden state `hвӮң = f(W_h В· hвӮңвӮӢвӮҒ + W_x В· xвӮң + b)`

The hidden state is supposed to carry a compressed summary of everything the RNN has seen so far.

```
"The"  вҶ’  "animal"  вҶ’  "didn't"  вҶ’  "cross"  вҶ’  "the"  вҶ’  "street"  вҶ’  ...  вҶ’  "tired"
 hвӮҒ         hвӮӮ          hвӮғ          hвӮ„         hвӮ…         hвӮҶ              hвӮҒвӮҖ
  в”Ӯ          в”Ӯ            в”Ӯ           в”Ӯ          в”Ӯ           в”Ӯ                в”Ӯ
  в””в”Җв”Җ hвӮҒ contains info about "The"
             в””в”Җв”Җ hвӮӮ contains info about "The" + "animal"
                           в””в”Җв”Җ hвӮғ tries to hold ALL previous words...
                                                                             в””в”Җв”Җ hвӮҒвӮҖ is supposed
                                                                                 to remember EVERYTHING
 в”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғв”Ғ вҶ’  time
```

#### The Fatal Flaw: Vanishing Information

**Problem:** The hidden state `h` is a **fixed-size** vector (e.g., 256 or 512 dimensions). You're trying to compress an *arbitrarily long* sentence into this fixed-size bottleneck. By the time the RNN reaches "tired" (position 10), the information about "animal" (position 2) has been **compressed, diluted, and partially overwritten** through 8 successive nonlinear transformations. This is the **vanishing information bottleneck**.

```
Information about "animal" retained at each step:

Step 2 (animal):  в–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳ  100%  вҶҗ Just processed it
Step 4 (cross):   в–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳ       80%  вҶҗ Starting to fade
Step 6 (street):  в–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳ             50%  вҶҗ Competing with newer words
Step 8 (was):     в–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳв–Ҳ                  25%  вҶҗ Badly diluted
Step 10 (tired):  в–Ҳв–Ҳ                     10%  вҶҗ Almost gone! рҹҳ°
```

This is closely related to the **vanishing gradient problem** вҖ” during backpropagation, gradients shrink exponentially as they travel backwards through many time steps, making it extremely hard for the model to learn long-range dependencies. Variants like **LSTMs** (Long Short-Term Memory) and **GRUs** (Gated Recurrent Units) introduced gating mechanisms to mitigate this, but they still fundamentally rely on a sequential chain of hidden states.

> рҹ“– **Further reading:**
> - Goodfellow, Bengio & Courville, *Deep Learning* (2016) вҖ” Chapter 10 covers RNNs, LSTMs, and the vanishing gradient problem in depth.
> - Jurafsky & Martin, *Speech and Language Processing* (3rd ed.) вҖ” Chapter 9 on RNNs and LSTMs, with a focus on NLP applications.
> - The original LSTM paper: Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, "Long Short-Term Memory" (1997).

### What Attention Does Differently

Instead of passing information through a chain, attention lets **every word directly look at every other word** in one step:

```
                    в”Ңв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”җ
                    в”Ӯ     DIRECT ACCESS             в”Ӯ
                    в”Ӯ                               в”Ӯ
    "The"  "animal"  "didn't"  "cross"  ...  "it"  ...  "tired"
                вҶ‘                              в”Ӯ
                в”Ӯ          NO CHAIN!            в”Ӯ
                в””в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”ҳ
                    "it" directly looks at
                    "animal" and scores it high
```

**Result:** No information loss. Every word has a **direct highway** to every other word, regardless of distance.

### 3.1 Token Embeddings вҖ” Turning Words into Numbers

Neural networks can't read text. Every word must be converted into a **vector of numbers** вҖ” a dense, continuous representation called an **embedding**. This is not a trivial encoding like one-hot vectors; embeddings carry **semantic meaning**.

#### Why Not Just Use One-Hot Vectors?

A naive approach would assign each word a unique binary vector:

```
 Vocabulary = {"I", "love", "ML", "hate", "python", ...}    (say 50,000 words)

 One-Hot:
   "I"    вҶ’ [1, 0, 0, 0, 0, ...]     (50,000-dim vector with a single 1)
   "love" вҶ’ [0, 1, 0, 0, 0, ...]
   "hate" вҶ’ [0, 0, 0, 1, 0, ...]
```

**Problems:**
1. **Astronomically large** вҖ” A 50K-word vocabulary means every word is a 50,000-dimensional vector. Multiply that by every word in every sentence in every batch... memory explodes.
2. **No notion of similarity** вҖ” The one-hot vectors for "love" and "adore" are just as far apart as "love" and "refrigerator". Dot product between *any* two different one-hot vectors is always exactly 0.
3. **Sparse and wasteful** вҖ” 99.998% of every vector is zeros.

#### Learned Embeddings

Instead, we learn a **dense, low-dimensional** vector for each word. The embedding table is a matrix of shape `(vocab_size Г— d_model)` that is trained alongside the rest of the model. During training, words that appear in similar contexts naturally end up with **similar vectors** вҖ” the famous insight behind Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013).

```

  "I"    вҶ’ token ID 0
  "love" вҶ’ token ID 1
  "ML"   вҶ’ token ID 2

  в”Ңв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”¬в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”җ
  в”Ӯ ID   в”Ӯ Embedding Vector (d=4)       в”Ӯ
  в”ңв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”јв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Ө
  в”Ӯ  0   в”Ӯ [ 1.0,  0.5,  0.3,  0.8 ]   в”Ӯ  вҶҗ "I"

### 3.2 Q, K, V вҖ” The Three Roles Every Token Plays

This is the heart of the attention mechanism and the most important conceptual leap. Here's the key insight: **every token simultaneously plays three roles**.

```
в”Ңв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”җ
в”Ӯ                     EVERY TOKEN IS...                           в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ                                                                 в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ  рҹ”Қ A QUERY  вҖ” "I'm looking for something"                     в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ                 (What information do I need from others?)        в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ                                                                 в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ  рҹҸ·пёҸ  A KEY    вҖ” "This is what I'm about"                        в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ                 (Here's my label so others can find me)          в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ                                                                 в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ  рҹ“Ұ A VALUE  вҖ” "This is the info I carry"                       в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ                 (If you select me, here's what you get)          в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ                                                                 в”Ӯ
в””в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”ҳ
```

#### Why Three Separate Projections?

You might wonder: "Why not just use the embedding directly?" If we computed `X В· XбөҖ` without any transformation, every token would always be **most similar to itself** (a vector's dot product with itself is its maximum). The attention would collapse into near-identity maps вҖ” every word mostly attends to itself, which is useless.

By projecting through three **separate, learnable** weight matrices, we allow the model to create three different **views** of the same token:

- **W_Q** learns to extract the aspect that says "here is what I *need*"
- **W_K** learns to extract the aspect that says "here is what I *offer*"
- **W_V** learns to extract the aspect that says "here is the actual *content* to pass along"

This is a **linear transformation** (also called a linear projection or affine map). Geometrically, a linear transformation can rotate, scale, shear, or reflect vectors in the embedding space. The weight matrices learn to reorient the token vectors so that tokens which *should* attend to each other end up with high dot products between their Q and K representations вҖ” even if their original embeddings are far apart.

For example, the pronoun "it" and the noun "cat" have very different embeddings, but W_Q and W_K can learn to project them into a shared subspace where `Q["it"] В· K["cat"]бөҖ` is large.

**How do we get Q, K, V?** Multiply the input X by three **separate learned weight matrices**:

```
Q = X В· W_Q        K = X В· W_K        V = X В· W_V
```

в”Ӯ 0.5  0.6  0.1  0.2   в”Ӯ      в”Ӯ 0.3  0.7  0.4  0.1   в”Ӯ      в”Ӯ 0.6  0.1  0.5  0.2   в”Ӯ
в”Ӯ 0.3  0.4  0.7  0.1   в”Ӯ      в”Ӯ 0.1  0.3  0.6  0.5   в”Ӯ      в”Ӯ 0.3  0.3  0.7  0.4   в”Ӯ

### 3.3 Dot Product вҖ” Measuring Similarity

Now we compute **how similar each Query is to each Key**. The tool: **dot product**.

#### What IS a Dot Product? (Quick Refresher)

The dot product of two vectors measures how **aligned** they are:

```
a = [1, 0]     b = [1, 0]     aВ·b = 1Г—1 + 0Г—0 = 1     вҶҗ Same direction (MAX)
a = [1, 0]     b = [0, 1]     aВ·b = 1Г—0 + 0Г—1 = 0     вҶҗ Perpendicular (ZERO)
a = [1, 0]     b = [-1, 0]    aВ·b = 1Г—(-1)+0Г—0 = -1   вҶҗ Opposite (MIN)
```

Visually:

```
   Same direction:          Perpendicular:          Opposite:
        b вҶ’                     b вҶ‘                    b вҶҗ
        a вҶ’                     a вҶ’                    a вҶ’
   dot = LARGE (+)          dot = ZERO              dot = LARGE (-)
   "very similar"           "unrelated"             "very different"
```

#### Computing Q В· KбөҖ

We need every Q dotted with every K. Matrix multiplication `Q В· KбөҖ` does this all at once:

```
Q В· KбөҖ = score matrix

              K_I     K_love    K_ML        вҶҗ Keys (columns)
         в”Ң                            в”җ

Q_ML     в”Ӯ  2.10     1.07      2.16   в”Ӯ     вҶҗ How much does "ML" attend to each word?
         в””                            в”ҳ
```

```

### 3.5 Softmax вҖ” From Raw Scores to Probabilities

Softmax is one of the most important functions in all of deep learning, not just attention. It converts a row of arbitrary real numbers (called **logits**) into a **probability distribution** вҖ” all values become positive and sum to 1. It appears everywhere: in classification heads, in attention weight computation, in language model output layers.

#### The Softmax Formula

```
softmax(zбөў) = exp(zбөў) / ОЈвұј exp(zвұј)
```

Or visually:

```
Input:   [ zвӮҒ,  zвӮӮ,  zвӮғ ]

Step 1 вҖ” Exponentiate each:
         [ e^zвӮҒ, e^zвӮӮ, e^zвӮғ ]      вҶҗ Makes everything positive
                                       (e^x > 0 for ALL x, even negative x)

Step 2 вҖ” Divide each by the sum:
         [ e^zвӮҒ/S, e^zвӮӮ/S, e^zвӮғ/S ]   where S = e^zвӮҒ + e^zвӮӮ + e^zвӮғ
                                         вҶҗ Forces sum = 1.0
```

**Why the exponential function?** The exponential `e^x` has two critical properties:
1. It's **always positive** вҖ” `e^(-100) = 0.0000...` (tiny but positive), `e^0 = 1`, `e^5 = 148.4`
2. It **preserves ordering** вҖ” if `zвӮҒ > zвӮӮ > zвӮғ`, then `e^zвӮҒ > e^zвӮӮ > e^zвӮғ`
3. It **amplifies differences** вҖ” small additive differences in logits become large multiplicative differences in probabilities, making the distribution "peaky"

These properties make softmax act like a **continuous, differentiable version of argmax** вҖ” the highest score gets the most probability mass, but unlike hard argmax, all positions get *some* weight and gradients can flow to all of them.

#### Worked Example: Softmax for "I"

```
Scaled scores for "I":  [1.00, 0.56, 1.09]

Step 1: Exponentiate
  e^1.00 = 2.718
  e^0.56 = 1.751
  e^1.09 = 2.974

Step 2: Sum
  S = 2.718 + 1.751 + 2.974 = 7.443

Step 3: Divide
  softmax = [2.718/7.443, 1.751/7.443, 2.974/7.443]
          = [0.365,       0.235,       0.399]

Check: 0.365 + 0.235 + 0.399 вүҲ 1.0 вң…
```

#### Temperature: Controlling the Sharpness

In practice, you'll often see a **temperature** parameter `П„` (tau) applied to softmax:

```
softmax(zбөў / П„)

  П„ = 1.0  вҶ’ standard softmax (default)
  П„ вҶ’ 0    вҶ’ approaches hard argmax (winner-take-all)
  П„ вҶ’ вҲһ    вҶ’ approaches uniform distribution (all equal)
```

The scaling by `1/вҲҡd_k` in attention is effectively a temperature of `П„ = вҲҡd_k`. This is why the scaling step (Section 3.4) and softmax are so intimately connected вҖ” scaling controls how "peaked" or "spread out" the attention distribution is.

### 3.6 Value Weighting вҖ” The Final Output

Now we use the attention weights to create a **weighted blend** of the Value vectors.

#### The Formula

```
Output = Attention_Weights Г— V
```

This means: for each token, its output is a **weighted average of all Value vectors**, where the weights come from the attention matrix.

#### Worked Example: Output for "I"

```
Attention weights for "I":  [0.365, 0.235, 0.399]

V_I    = [0.57, 0.91, 0.52, 0.92]
V_love = [0.77, 0.38, 0.77, 0.38]
V_ML   = [0.69, 0.78, 0.71, 0.88]

Output_I = 0.365 Г— V_I + 0.235 Г— V_love + 0.399 Г— V_ML
```

Let's compute dimension by dimension:

```
dim 1:  0.365Г—0.57 + 0.235Г—0.77 + 0.399Г—0.69 = 0.208 + 0.181 + 0.275 = 0.664

## 5. Masking: Hiding the Future

To understand masking, we first need to understand the **encoder-decoder architecture** and why "hiding the future" matters.

### Background: Encoder vs Decoder

The original Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) has two major components:

```

**Encoder** вҖ” Processes the full input sequence bidirectionally. Every token can "see" every other token. This is ideal for tasks like understanding/classification ("What is the sentiment of this sentence?"), because the model has access to all context.

**Decoder** вҖ” Generates output tokens one at a time, left-to-right. During training, we feed the entire target sequence at once (for efficiency), but we must **prevent** each position from attending to future positions. Why? Because at inference time, those future tokens haven't been generated yet! If the model learned to rely on seeing the future during training, it would be useless at inference.

This is where **causal masking** (also called "look-ahead masking") comes in.

### Without Mask (Encoder вҖ” BERT style)

Every word sees every other word вҖ” **bidirectional** attention:

```
         "I"    "love"   "ML"
"I"     [ вң…     вң…       вң…  ]    вҶҗ "I" can see the whole sentence
"love"  [ вң…     вң…       вң…  ]    вҶҗ "love" can see everything
"ML"    [ вң…     вң…       вң…  ]    вҶҗ "ML" can see everything
```

This is perfect for tasks where you have the complete input (e.g., text classification, question answering over a passage), but it **cannot be used for text generation** because it would be "cheating" вҖ” looking at answers before generating them.

### With Causal Mask (Decoder вҖ” GPT style)

Each word can only see itself and **past** words вҖ” **autoregressive** attention:

```
         "I"    "love"   "ML"
"I"     [ вң…     вқҢ       вқҢ  ]    вҶҗ "I" can only see itself
"love"  [ вң…     вң…       вқҢ  ]    вҶҗ "love" sees "I" and itself
"ML"    [ вң…     вң…       вң…  ]    вҶҗ "ML" sees everything before it
```

The mask is a **lower-triangular matrix** вҖ” it forms a triangle where information only flows from left to right, never backwards. This ensures the model's predictions at position `t` depend only on positions `1, 2, ..., t`, exactly mimicking what happens during autoregressive generation.

### How It Works Mechanically

We add **-вҲһ** (negative infinity) to the masked positions BEFORE softmax:

```
Scaled scores:               After masking:               After softmax:
в”Ң 1.00  0.56  1.09 в”җ        в”Ң 1.00   -вҲһ    -вҲһ  в”җ        в”Ң 1.00   0.00   0.00 в”җ
в”Ӯ 0.62  0.49  0.69 в”Ӯ   вҶ’    в”Ӯ 0.62  0.49   -вҲһ  в”Ӯ   вҶ’    в”Ӯ 0.53   0.47   0.00 в”Ӯ
в”” 1.05  0.54  1.08 в”ҳ        в”” 1.05  0.54  1.08 в”ҳ        в”” 0.37   0.22   0.38 в”Ӯ

Why -вҲһ?  Because e^(-вҲһ) = 0, so softmax gives those positions exactly 0 weight.
```

```

### Other Types of Masks

Causal masking isn't the only masking pattern. Here are others you'll encounter:

| Mask Type | Description | Used In |
|---|---|---|
| **Causal (look-ahead)** | Lower-triangular; prevents seeing the future | GPT, LLaMA, decoder-only models |
| **Padding mask** | Prevents attending to `[PAD]` tokens in variable-length batches | All Transformer models |
| **MLM mask** | Randomly masks 15% of tokens for prediction | BERT pre-training |
| **Prefix mask** | Bidirectional for a prefix, causal for the rest | Prefix-LM, T5, PaLM |
| **Sliding window** | Each token attends only to a local window | Longformer, Mistral |

## 6. Multi-Head Attention: Looking at Many Things at Once

Multi-head attention is arguably the single most important architectural innovation in the Transformer. It's the difference between a model that can understand one thing about a sentence and a model that can simultaneously understand *many* things.

### The Problem with Single-Head Attention

A single attention head computes **one** attention matrix вҖ” one set of weights that determines how much each token attends to each other token. But language encodes **many simultaneous relationships** between words:

```
"The cat sat on the mat because it was tired"

  Relationship 1 (syntactic):   "cat" вҶ’ "sat"      (subject-verb)
  Relationship 2 (coreference): "it"  вҶ’ "cat"      (pronoun reference)
  Relationship 3 (modifier):    "tired" вҶ’ "it/cat"  (adjective-noun)
  Relationship 4 (spatial):     "on" вҶ’ "mat"        (preposition-object)
```

A single attention matrix can't capture all of these at once! The attention weights for "it" would need to simultaneously point strongly to "cat" (for coreference) AND to "tired" (for modifier resolution). With one set of weights that must sum to 1, this creates an impossible tension вҖ” the model has to pick one relationship or blur them together.

### The Solution: Multiple Heads

The insight is deceptively simple: **run multiple attention operations in parallel**, each in a smaller subspace, and combine the results. Each "head" can specialize in detecting a different type of linguistic relationship.

Split the embedding into **multiple smaller "heads"**, each running attention independently:

```

### Step by Step

```
Step 1: Project input into h separate Q, K, V sets
        в”Ңв”Җ QвӮҒ, KвӮҒ, VвӮҒ  (each d_k = 64)
   X в”Җв”Җв”Җв”јв”Җ QвӮӮ, KвӮӮ, VвӮӮ  (each d_k = 64)
        в”ңв”Җ ...
        в””в”Җ QвӮҲ, KвӮҲ, VвӮҲ  (each d_k = 64)

Step 2: Run attention INDEPENDENTLY on each head
        headвӮҒ = Attention(QвӮҒ, KвӮҒ, VвӮҒ)   вҶ’ outputвӮҒ (shape: seq_len Г— 64)

        concat = [outputвӮҒ ; outputвӮӮ ; ... ; outputвӮҲ]

        MultiHead_Output = concat Г— W_O
        shape: (seq_len Г— 512)
```

### Why Concatenate + Project (W_O)?

The concatenation + output projection (W_O) is not just bookkeeping вҖ” it serves a critical purpose. After concatenation, we have a 512-dimensional vector where the first 64 dims come from Head 1, the next 64 from Head 2, etc. The output projection W_O (a 512Г—512 learned matrix) allows the model to **mix information across heads**. Without W_O, each head's output would remain isolated in its own subspace. With W_O, the model can learn combinations like "if Head 3 detected a subject-verb relationship AND Head 4 detected coreference, then the output should encode X."

Interestingly, Voita et al. also found that **many heads are redundant** вҖ” you can prune a significant fraction of heads (sometimes 60вҖ“80%) with minimal performance loss. This has implications for model compression (a topic deeply relevant to this thesis!).

### Key Insight: No Extra Parameters!

```
Single head:    W_Q is (512 Г— 512)  = 262,144 params
8 heads:        Each W_Q is (512 Г— 64)  = 32,768 params Г— 8 = 262,144 params

Same total parameters! We just SPLIT the computation into parallel tracks.
The only added cost is W_O (512 Г— 512 = 262,144 params) for the output projection.
```

This is a remarkable design choice вҖ” multi-head attention is essentially **free** in terms of parameter count compared to single-head attention of the same total dimension. The computational cost is also nearly identical because the matrix multiplications can be batched. The only overhead is the output projection W_O.

### Why Cross-Attention Matters

Self-attention answers: *"What relationships exist within my own sequence?"*

Cross-attention answers a fundamentally different question: *"What parts of the source input are relevant for generating my current output?"*

Without cross-attention, an encoder-decoder model would have no mechanism for the decoder to "look at" the input. The encoder would process the source and produce rich representations, but the decoder would have no way to access them. Cross-attention is the **bridge** between encoder and decoder.

In the original Transformer, each decoder layer has **three** sub-layers:
1. **Masked self-attention** вҖ” the decoder attends to its own (past) tokens
2. **Cross-attention** вҖ” the decoder attends to the encoder's output
3. **Feed-forward network** вҖ” a position-wise MLP

The cross-attention layer receives K and V from the encoder's final output, and Q from the decoder's self-attention output. Crucially, the encoder's K and V are computed **once** and reused for every decoder step during generation вҖ” this is a major efficiency win.

### Cross-Attention Example: Translation

```
Source (English): "I love ML"        вҶ’  Encoder  вҶ’  K, V
Target (French):  "J'adore le ML"    вҶ’  Decoder  вҶ’  Q

Cross-attention lets each French word "look at" the English sentence
to decide which English words are relevant for generating the next French word.

              English Keys
           "I"   "love"   "ML"
French Q  в”Ңв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”җ
"J'"      в”Ӯ 0.7    0.2    0.1   в”Ӯ  вҶҗ "J'" mostly looks at "I"
"adore"   в”Ӯ 0.1    0.8    0.1   в”Ӯ  вҶҗ "adore" mostly looks at "love"
"le"      в”Ӯ 0.1    0.1    0.1   в”Ӯ  вҶҗ "le" is a function word (spreads attention)
"ML"      в”Ӯ 0.05   0.05   0.9   в”Ӯ  вҶҗ "ML" attends to "ML"
          в””в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”ҳ
```

Notice how the cross-attention matrix is **not square** вҖ” it's (target_length Г— source_length). The French sequence has 4 tokens and the English sequence has 3 tokens, giving a 4Г—3 attention matrix. Each row still sums to 1 (it's a probability distribution over the source), but the matrix is rectangular.

## 8. Common Confusions Clarified

### вқ“ "Why do we need separate Q, K, V? Why not just use the input directly?"

```
WITHOUT Q, K, V (just using X):
  score = X В· XбөҖ
  This ALWAYS makes a token most similar to ITSELF (dot product with itself is max).
  The attention would mostly be self-referential вҶ’ useless.

WITH Q, K, V (different projections):
  Q extracts "what am I looking for?"
  K extracts "what do I contain?"
  These are DIFFERENT aspects of the same token.
  Now "it" (as a Query) can match strongly with "cat" (as a Key),
  even though "it" and "cat" have very different embeddings.
```

### вқ“ "Why softmax and not just normalization (divide by sum)?"

```
Simple normalization:   weights = scores / sum(scores)
  Problem: Doesn't handle negative scores!
  scores = [-5, 3, 1]  вҶ’  sum = -1  вҶ’  weights = [5, -3, -1]  вҶҗ Negative weights! рҹ’Җ

Softmax:              weights = exp(scores) / sum(exp(scores))
  exp() makes everything positive first, THEN normalizes.
  scores = [-5, 3, 1]  вҶ’  exp = [0.007, 20.1, 2.72]  вҶ’  weights = [0.0003, 0.88, 0.12] вң…
```

### вқ“ "Does attention have memory across sequences?"

```
NO! Attention only operates WITHIN a single input sequence.

Sequence 1: "I love ML"         вҶ’  attention within these 3 tokens only
Sequence 2: "Transformers rock"  вҶ’  attention within these 2 tokens only

These two sequences NEVER interact via attention.
(That's what context windows and positional embeddings are for.)
```

### вқ“ "What's the computational cost?"

### Foundational Textbooks

| Book | Authors | Why Read It |
|---|---|---|
| *Deep Learning* (2016) | Goodfellow, Bengio & Courville | The "bible" of deep learning. Covers RNNs, LSTMs, vanishing gradients, softmax, and the mathematical foundations. Available free at [deeplearningbook.org](https://www.deeplearningbook.org). |
| *Speech and Language Processing* (3rd ed.) | Jurafsky & Martin | The definitive NLP textbook. Covers embeddings, RNNs, Transformers, and attention from an NLP perspective. Draft available free at [web.stanford.edu/~jurafsky/slp3/](https://web.stanford.edu/~jurafsky/slp3/). |
| *Neural Network Methods for Natural Language Processing* (2017) | Yoav Goldberg | Excellent bridge between NLP and deep learning. Particularly strong on word embeddings and sequence models. |
| *Deep Learning: Foundations and Concepts* (2024) | Bishop & Bishop | Modern, comprehensive treatment of deep learning with clear mathematical exposition. Chapter 12 covers Transformers. |
| *Formal Algorithms for Transformers* (2022) | Phuong & Hutter | A concise, mathematically precise pseudocode specification of every Transformer component. Ideal for implementation. |
